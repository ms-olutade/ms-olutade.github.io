An Orientation

The education we offer is wholly Orthodox Christian. It is ancient. It is innovative. And it is ours.

At ICON, students traverse the classical trivium — the grammar, logic, and rhetoric of every subject — while being formed within the living tradition of the Coptic Orthodox Church. This is not a secular curriculum with religion added on like apparel, nor a catechism program borrowing academic subjects as ornament. It is a single coherent formation in which Scripture, liturgy, patristic literature, mathematics, the sciences, the humanities, and the sacred arts are taught as one continuous inquiry into the order of the world and the life of the Church that inhabits it.

Students move through three developmental stages that shape the whole curriculum. In the Grammar stage (Grades 4–5), they acquire the vocabulary, narratives, and habits of each discipline. In the Logic stage (Grades 6–8), they learn to analyze, to argue, to trace cause and form. In the Rhetoric stage (Grades 9–12), they learn to compose — in prose, in proof, in chant, in icon, in sermon — and to offer what they have received back to the Church and the world.

What follows is an account of what we teach and why, organized by department. Every course is designed with two horizons in view: how it builds on what came before in the student's life, and how it speaks to what they are learning this same year in every other subject. This is what curriculum alignment means at ICON, and it is the work that most distinguishes us from programs that teach subjects in parallel isolation.

II
Departments & Progressions

The Disciplines in Detail

Each department is introduced by a statement of its aim, followed by the developmental arc it follows from Grade 4 through Grade 12, and a course-by-course description of what students encounter in each year.

i.

Biblical, Liturgical & Theological Studies

BLT is the spine of the ICON curriculum. Its aim is seminary-grade biblical fluency, deep patristic formation, and the capacity to read, teach, and preach within the living tradition of the Coptic Orthodox Church.

Every student traverses the entire Bible each year through a different developmental lens — nine lenses across nine years. The annual traversal is designed to complete what the liturgy already assumes: the Coptic Katameros saturates students in the New Testament but concentrates Old Testament coverage in Lent and Holy Week, so our program ensures the whole of Scripture becomes interior furniture for every graduate. Patristic study begins in Grade 4 with lives of the saints, matures in Grade 8 with Athanasius's On the Incarnation, and culminates in Grade 12 with contemporary Coptic fathers — Pope Shenouda III, Fr. Matta El Meskeen, their interlocutors and a reread of the Life of St. Anthony, which students first encountered in 9th grade. Homiletics scaffolds from oral retelling in the Grammar years to the public preaching of assigned lectionary texts by Grade 12.

Grammar

Grades 4–5 · Narrative Traversal

Students read the Bible as story — memorizing the shape of its chapters, the cast of its characters, the movement of its arc from creation through Christ and into the Church.

Logic

Grades 6–8 · Guided Traversal

Students read the Bible with the Church — through the lens of liturgy, typology, and the voices of the early Fathers. Athanasius's On the Incarnation anchors Grade 8.

Rhetoric

Grades 9–12 · Full Traversal

Students read the Bible as future teachers — tracing themes, practicing exegesis, synthesizing dogma, and by the senior year preaching the lectionary before their peers.

Major biblical themes traced across both Testaments: covenant, exile, kingdom, sacrifice, resurrection. Students begin to see Scripture as a theologically coherent whole and acquire the grammar of systematic reasoning about God.

The disciplines of biblical interpretation: grammatical, historical, canonical, and typological reading. Students learn to work with Greek lexica, consult patristic commentary, and write exegetical papers on assigned pericopes.

A systematic account of Orthodox doctrine with reference to contested questions in the modern Christian landscape, including readings from Pope Shenouda III's Comparative Theology and Matta El Meskeen on spiritual life.

Preaching the Lectionary

Each senior is assigned a sequence of Katameros readings and delivers prepared sermons on them throughout the year before peers and faculty. Training in exegesis, composition, delivery, and the ethics of pastoral speech. The capstone of the nine-year BLT arc.

ii.

Humanities & History

The study of history at ICON is the study of how peoples and places have sought — and often failed — to live well together, and of the particular civilization that Christ entered and that His Church has since traversed.

In the Grammar stage, students inhabit places before they periodize them. A two-year Cities sequence introduces the disciplines of the humanities — historical, geographical, political, economic — first through the city and the local community, then through the cradle of the student's own ecclesial inheritance: the Coptic and Byzantine Christian world. In the Logic stage, students take a three-year world-history sequence: Ancient, Medieval, Early Modern. In the Rhetoric stage, they take the full Advanced Placement sequence in Human Geography, World History, US History, and Economics.

iii.

English & Literature

At ICON, students learn to write with structure and to read with the whole human race. The writing curriculum is built on the Institute for Excellence in Writing — a sequenced training in the classical arts of imitation, paraphrase, and composition. The reading curriculum, which we call World Classics, is a deliberate departure from the standard classical school list of Anglo-Christian authors.

Most classical schools teach a narrow Western literature as if it were the only literature worth studying, that the world at large has offered nothing of note. We teach instead a global canon of classics— Greek tragedy, Coptic monastic literature, Syriac poetry, Russian novels, Chinese science fiction, Latin American magical realism, West African plays, post-modern American prose, works in translation, alongside the Shakespeare, Tolkien, Lewis, etc. The guiding principle is that the Orthodox Church is catholic — universal — and her students should know something of how the human condition has been depicted, sung, processed, and narrated across the places God has made. Students read to experience Creation's groaning for the Creator -- to witness, through a thousand frames, how God's redemptive breath is drawing all humanity unto Himself. The full World Classics reading list is published separately and is curated by the English faculty in consultation with the school's broader academic and spiritual council.

iv.

Mathematics

Mathematics at ICON is taught as a liberal art — not as a toolkit, not as test preparation, but as the discipline in which the human mind most clearly apprehends form. Our sequence is designed so that every graduate reaches college-level calculus or statistics and has the option to go further.

We use Singapore's Dimensions Math from Grade 4 through Grade 8 because its approach — concrete, pictorial, then abstract — is faithful to how children actually acquire mathematical intuition. In the upper school we move to the textbooks long preferred by mathematicians who teach: Harold Jacobs for Geometry, Paul Foerster for Algebra II, Trigonometry, and Pre-Calculus. Geometry in Grade 9 is paired deliberately with the start of formal Rhetoric and the continued study of Logic, because Euclidean proof is the most accessible form of formal deductive reasoning a young person encounters. Enrichment through Math Olympiad, AMC, and directed problem-solving is available at every level from Grade 5 onward.

v.

Science

Science at ICON begins with wonder and issues into rigor. In the Grammar years, students work with Mystery Science — a phenomenon-based curriculum that teaches young students to observe carefully and ask the kinds of questions that lead to investigation. In the Logic years, students move into Issues & Science, the middle-school program published by the Lawrence Hall of Science at UC Berkeley.

We chose Issues & Science deliberately. It is one of the very few middle-school science programs to receive an all-green rating from EdReports — the independent reviewer of K–12 curricula — for full alignment to all dimensions of the Next Generation Science Standards. The program organizes learning around real-world scientific issues that students investigate as scientists do, building disciplinary knowledge in the service of genuine inquiry. The high-school sequence then follows the traditional Earth-Bio-Chem-Physics ladder, with Advanced Placement options available to students at each level. Laboratory work is central in every year of the program.

vi.

Languages

Every ICON student studies Greek. Greek is the language of the New Testament, of the Septuagint from which the Church Fathers most often read, and of the Alexandrian theological tradition that shaped the Coptic Church. It is not offered as an option; it is the common tongue of our study.

Coptic — the liturgical and heritage language of the Coptic Orthodox Church — is available from Grade 4 as an elective and is taken by most students whose families are rooted in the Coptic tradition. Beginning in Grade 6, students also elect a modern language (Spanish, Arabic or Chinese), which they pursue through graduation. Strong students graduate trilingually literate: fluent in ancient Greek sufficient to read patristic prose, conversant in their chosen modern language, and — for heritage students — competent in Coptic for liturgical participation.

Greek I through V

Five years of sequential Attic and Koine Greek. Students acquire the inflectional system, a working vocabulary, and facility with unvocalized liturgical and scriptural texts. By Grade 8 students read selections from the Gospels and the Divine Liturgy of Saint Basil in Greek.

An upper-level seminar in a patristic or scriptural author, followed by a senior-year directed translation project. Students produce a substantial translation of their own, submitted with a critical introduction and philological notes.

A sequential program in Bohairic Coptic offered as an elective from Grade 4 through graduation. Students learn to read the liturgy, the lectionary, and selected hymnography in the language of their tradition.

A seven-year sequential program in a modern language chosen at the beginning of Grade 6. Students prepare for Advanced Placement examinations in their chosen language by the end of Grade 12.

vii.

Logic & Rhetoric

The second and third arts of the trivium are taught explicitly across six years, beginning with Pre-Logic in the late Grammar stage and culminating with Rhetoric II in the early Rhetoric stage. The progression moves a student from learning to think clearly, to learning to argue formally, to learning to speak persuasively and well. Beyond Grade 10, rhetorical training continues implicitly: in AP English Language, in the dogmatic argumentation of BLT Synthesis, and in the Homiletics capstone of the senior year.

5
Pre-Logic I

Monday & Friday, throughout the year

An age-appropriate introduction to clear thinking: the recognition of arguments, the distinction between premises and conclusions, the identification of common informal fallacies. Designed to give Grammar-stage students the vocabulary they will need when formal logic begins.

6
Pre-Logic II

Monday & Friday, throughout the year

Continuation of Pre-Logic I with attention to definitions, classification, and the structure of categorical statements. Students prepare for the formal study of syllogistic reasoning that begins in Grade 7.

7
Introduction to Logic I

Canon Press, Introductory Logic

The first year of Canon Press's Introductory Logic: terms, definitions, propositions, and the categorical syllogism. Students learn to identify, construct, and evaluate formal arguments.

8
Introduction to Logic II

Canon Press, Introductory Logic, continued

The second year of Introductory Logic: deeper work with syllogistic and the entry into propositional reasoning, including the formal detection of fallacies and the assessment of argument validity.

9
Rhetoric I

James Nance, Fitting Words · Canon Press

An introduction to the classical rhetorical tradition: invention, arrangement, and style. Students study the canons of rhetoric and begin to compose short orations of their own. Paired with Geometry in the same year for the mutual reinforcement of formal reasoning across modalities.

10
Rhetoric II

James Nance, Fitting Words, continued

Advanced rhetorical training, including the rhetorical analysis of patristic and classical texts. Students prepare longer compositions and deliver them publicly. The course closes the formal sequence in Logic and Rhetoric and prepares students for AP English Language and the Homiletics capstone that follow.

11–12
Rhetoric in Application

After Grade 10, formal rhetoric continues implicitly across the curriculum: in the rhetorical analysis and composition of AP English Language, in the dogmatic argumentation of the BLT Synthesis course, and in the public preaching of assigned Katameros texts in the senior year's Homiletics capstone.

viii.

Sacred Arts

Iconography and chant are not ornaments to the curriculum. They are among its central disciplines. An icon is written theology; a hymn is sung doctrine. In studying them, students learn to make — with their hands and their voices — the same tradition they are reading in BLT and encountering in the liturgy each week.

Every student studies iconography for nine years. In Grades 4–7, Iconography Foundations introduces the grammar of sacred image: the canonical proportions, the pigments, the gestures, the theology. In Grades 8–12, the Iconography Studio takes students through progressively more demanding commissions, culminating in a senior capstone icon presented to the school community. Coptic and Byzantine chant is studied continuously from Grade 4 through graduation, with students learning to serve liturgically by Grade 8 and to lead congregational chant by Grade 12.

4–7Foundations
Iconography Foundations

Four years of preparatory work: copying, pigment preparation, board and gesso work, and the reading of icons as theological statements. Students produce finished studies by the end of Grade 7.

8–11Studio
Iconography Studio I–IV

Progressive studio years. Students undertake annual commissions of increasing scope and complexity, working in egg tempera on gessoed board according to the canonical tradition. Critique and consultation with visiting iconographers are part of the studio year.

12
Senior Capstone Commission

Each senior undertakes a substantial icon commissioned by the school or by a parish, completed over the course of the year and accompanied by a written account of its iconographic sources and theological content.

4–12Chant
Coptic & Byzantine Chant

Nine years of continuous chant instruction. Students learn the modal systems, the hymns of the Coptic Midnight Praises (Tasbeha), and the music of the Divine Liturgy. By Grade 8, students serve liturgically; by Grade 12, they lead congregational chant in parish settings.

ix.

Physical Formation

Physical education at ICON is designed as formation, not activity. We ask the body to learn what the mind and soul are also learning: discipline, courage, teamwork, humility, and the stewardship of the gifts God has given.

Our physical program combines team basketball, strength and conditioning, flexibility and mobility training, self-defense, and outdoor education. Because our campus sits within a corporate neighborhood without immediate outdoor access, outdoor education occurs on a distinct rhythm: quarterly school-wide hikes that include families, alongside longer expeditions in the upper grades. By high school, students elect their own mix of team athletics and individual training.

4–5Foundations
Physical Education: Foundations

Movement literacy, coordination, flexibility, and the foundations of strength. Introduction to basketball fundamentals and to the quarterly family hike tradition.

6–8Team & Self
PE: Team Sports & Self-Defense

Competitive basketball, the other half-court sports accessible in our facility, and a self-defense sequence taught with attention to de-escalation, personal responsibility, and the ethics of physical force.

9–12Athletics
Athletics & Strength & Conditioning

Upper-school students elect a combination of competitive team athletics (basketball) and a programmed strength-and-conditioning curriculum. Outdoor education weekends and family hikes continue on the quarterly rhythm.

This is what classical curriculum alignment looks like in practice: the student reading Athanasius in BLT is simultaneously reading him in Greek, meeting his historical moment in Humanities, and learning the genre of theological argument in English — all in the same eight months of the same year. No subject is taught in isolation from the others. This is the discipline the faculty undertakes each summer in joint planning, and it is the single feature of an ICON education that cannot be replicated by a curriculum package alone.
IV
The ICON Diploma

Graduation Requirements & Portrait

What a graduate carries into the world, and what the diploma formally attests.

Biblical, Liturgical & Theological Studies

Nine years (Grades 4–12). The full nine-lens annual biblical traversal, culminating in the senior Homiletics capstone.

Mathematics

Nine years through AP Calculus BC or AP Statistics. AP Statistics also available concurrently in Grades 10 and 11 for advanced students.

Science

Nine years across the Mystery Science, Issues & Science, and Earth-Bio-Chem-Physics ladder, with AP options at the upper levels.

Humanities & History

Nine years, including the full AP sequence: Human Geography, World History: Modern, US History, and Economics (Micro and Macro).

English & Literature

Nine years of writing and reading through the World Classics sequence, including AP English Language and the Senior Seminar in World Literature.

Languages

Nine years of Greek, plus seven years of a modern language beginning in Grade 6. Coptic elective available throughout.

Logic & Rhetoric

Six years of formal training: Pre-Logic (Grades 5–6), Introduction to Logic (Grades 7–8), and Rhetoric (Grades 9–10). Continued application through Grade 12.

Sacred Arts

Nine years of continuous instruction in iconography and chant, culminating in a senior capstone icon and demonstrated competence in liturgical chant leadership.

Physical Formation

Nine years of physical education, team athletics, strength & conditioning, and outdoor education.

Senior Capstones

Three capstone works are produced in the senior year: a sermon series (Homiletics), a substantial icon (Sacred Arts), and an analytical essay (Senior Seminar). Together they constitute the public defense of the ICON education.

Portrait of a Graduate

A graduate of ICON leaves the school prepared to take up a place in the Church and the world — as a student, a neighbor, a citizen, a servant.

Has read the whole Bible nine times in nine ways and can locate any passage within the sweep of salvation history.
Reads patristic Greek sufficient to work from the original and understands the theological grammar of the Church Fathers.
Serves liturgically and can lead congregational chant, having been formed in the music of the Church since Grade 4.
Has written icons in the canonical tradition and understands sacred image as theology made visible.
Argues with clarity and charity, having studied formal logic, rhetoric, and the preaching of the Word.
Reasons quantitatively through calculus or advanced statistics and knows what mathematical proof asks of a mind.
Has read widely across world literatures and can locate the human condition in traditions beyond their own.
Takes up the body's disciplines of strength, coordination, and outdoor competence as part of a whole human life.
