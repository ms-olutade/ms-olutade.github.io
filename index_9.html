An Orientation

The education we offer is wholly Eastern Orthodox. It is ancient. It is innovative. And it is ours.

At All Saints Orthodox Academy, students traverse the classical trivium — the grammar, logic, and rhetoric of every subject — while being formed within the living tradition of the Eastern Orthodox Church. This is not a secular curriculum with religion added on like apparel, nor a catechism program borrowing academic subjects as ornament. It is a single coherent formation in which Scripture, liturgy, patristic literature, mathematics, the sciences, the humanities, and the sacred arts are taught as one continuous inquiry into the order of the world and the life of the Church that inhabits it.

All Saints is Eastern Orthodox in its ecclesial identity and liturgical life, and draws freely from the full breadth of the Orthodox patrimony — Greek, Byzantine, Slavic, and Antiochian alike — the common inheritance of the one undivided Church of the first millennium and the Fathers who taught within it.

Students move through three developmental stages that shape the whole curriculum. In the Grammar stage (Grades 4–5), they acquire the vocabulary, narratives, and habits of each discipline. In the Logic stage (Grades 6–8), they learn to analyze, to argue, to trace cause and form. In the Rhetoric stage (Grades 9–12), they learn to compose — in prose, in proof, in chant, in icon, in sermon — and to offer what they have received back to the Church and the world.

What follows is an account of what we teach and why, organized by department. Every course is designed with two horizons in view: how it builds on what came before in the student's life, and how it speaks to what they are learning this same year in every other subject. This is what curriculum alignment means at All Saints, and it is the work that most distinguishes us from programs that teach subjects in parallel isolation.

II
Why Now, Why Here

The Adolescent Years, Held with Care

All Saints Orthodox Academy begins in Grade 4 and runs through Grade 12 for a reason. The years between ten and eighteen are the years in which identity is formed — and the years in which the world is most organized to unform it. The case for All Saints is the case for giving the Church a serious institutional presence in those years.

The early-childhood years are, for most of our families, already held with remarkable care. Orthodox parents and the parish invest deeply in the youngest children — Sunday school, the cycle of feasts, children's liturgies, the rhythms of home. By the time a child reaches fourth grade, the patterns of faith are largely laid. What changes after that is not the capacity for formation but the pressure against it.

The middle and high school years are when every social and digital and commercial force an adolescent encounters begins to argue against the formation their family and Church have given them. The peer group enlarges. The phone arrives. The questions about identity become sharp — questions about the body, about belonging, about ambition, about what a good life even looks like. It is the most formative span of years in a human life, and for this generation it is also the most contested. A parish youth meeting once a week is not enough institutional weight to hold the ground.

This is why All Saints exists, and why it exists for these grades. The school is a serious claim on the student's weekdays during the years when a serious claim is what the student most needs — not an optional enrichment atop an already-formed life, but the primary institution in which the formation happens. The aim is to steer, with the full knowledge that no school can control an adolescent and no school should try. What All Saints offers during these years is the company, the texts, the questions, the disciplines, and the adults who can walk alongside a young person through a treacherous stretch until their identity has taken on a confident shape.

This is what the Fathers knew when they built the catechumenate, what the monastic schools knew when they educated the Byzantine world, and what every serious religious tradition has known in every century: the adolescent years are the years in which the faith is either taken up for oneself or quietly set down. The whole argument of All Saints is that the Church's institutional energy belongs now in the adolescent years — where it serves the parish as its strongest and most sustained ally in the work that matters most.

Small by Design

Five to six students per grade in the founding year

All Saints opens with cohorts of five to six students per grade. Every student is known by name in the first week of school. Every essay is read carefully by the teacher who assigned it. Every question a student raises in BLT can be taken seriously in the same class session — and often in the next subject's class, because the faculty are in conversation with each other across the week.

As the school grows, cohort sizes will expand deliberately, with an upper limit designed to preserve the essential character of small-group instruction. A class of six to twelve is the size at which discussion is possible and attention is achievable, which is what the classical method requires.

Faithful Faculty

Teachers who are Orthodox themselves

The faculty of All Saints are highly qualified teachers with advanced degrees in education, in their subject areas, and in theology, drawn from our own local Eastern Orthodox communities — practicing parishioners whose daily lives are already rooted in the tradition the curriculum transmits. This matters because formation is relational. A student learns patristic theology from a teacher whose own life has been shaped by the same texts — more than from the text alone.

The Full Curriculum

Nine years of serious college preparation

All Saints is a classical school, and it is also a college-preparatory school. Our graduates leave with the full AP sequence in the humanities (Human Geography, World History, US History, Economics, English Language), AP options in the sciences (Biology, Chemistry, Physics C) and mathematics (Calculus BC, Statistics), nine years of Greek, and seven years of a modern language.

They also leave with a transcript unlike almost any other applicant's. A patristic Greek translation, a substantial icon, a public sermon series, a capstone essay on world literature — these are artifacts of intellectual seriousness that college admissions offices meet almost nowhere else. The graduate arrives at college unusually formed.

Real Work in the World

Professional internships that matter

Beginning in Grade 11, All Saints aims to place students in professional internships matched to their developing interests and to the school's own intellectual gravity. Early conversations are underway with the Catena Bible app, a project devoted to making the patristic interpretive tradition digitally accessible — work that could offer students a rare opportunity to combine data labeling and NLP with deep patristic study and the preservation of the Church's interpretive tradition. Concrete program details will develop as these partnerships mature.

Additional placements are being pursued through parish, foundation, and professional networks, with an aim toward opportunities in publishing, theology, law, medicine, engineering, and the arts. This is project-based learning at its most serious: work that matters, in which a student's labor contributes to something that outlives the school year.

The Rhythms of Community

Prosphora, trails, and the soup kitchen

One or two weekends each month are given to the life the school shares outside its classrooms: prosphora baking at the parish with a teacher and students together preparing the liturgical bread; cohort hikes and outdoor expeditions that bring the student's body into contact with the Creation they are studying; and regular community service projects organized through the school's own network of Orthodox parishes across the region.

These are not extracurriculars. They are the formation of a person who knows how to bake for the Church, how to walk for a day with friends, and how to hand a meal to a stranger — which is to say, a person in whom the works of mercy have become second nature.

Uniform & Presence

A simple, affordable dress code

The uniform at All Saints is deliberately minimal: a white or navy collared shirt (short or long sleeve), khaki or navy pants, and closed-toe shoes.

The uniform is purchasable at any standard retailer for well under a hundred dollars per student. The aim is to remove a daily distraction — so that what the student wears in the morning is a quiet indication that they are here and ready to work.

The Campus

Rooted at St. Basil's in San Juan Capistrano, in partnership with the Eastern Orthodox parishes of Orange County.

All Saints Orthodox Academy is establishing its founding home at St. Basil's in San Juan Capistrano, a parish of the Eastern Orthodox Church, held in close partnership with Orthodox parishes across Orange County. To be formed within the life of a parish — to learn in the same place one worships — is not incidental to the school's design but central to it.

San Juan Capistrano anchors the school in the historic and liturgical life of South Orange County, within range of families across the county and into north San Diego County. The parish setting gives students daily proximity to the Church's worship, its sacred space, and its rhythms — the living context in which the whole curriculum is meant to be received.

Students will begin every school day with Morning Prayer. The Divine Liturgy will be available to students on feast days as part of the school day, not as an extraction from it — celebrated in the church at St. Basil's where the students learn, so that the work of the classroom and the prayer of the Church remain one motion.

III
Departments & Progressions

The Disciplines in Detail

Each department is introduced by a statement of its aim, followed by the developmental arc it follows from Grade 4 through Grade 12, and a course-by-course description of what students encounter in each year.

i.

Biblical, Liturgical & Theological Studies

BLT is the spine of the All Saints curriculum. Its aim is seminary-grade biblical fluency, deep patristic formation, and the capacity to read, teach, and preach within the living tradition of the Eastern Orthodox Church.

Every student traverses the entire Bible each year through a different developmental lens — nine lenses across nine years. The annual traversal is designed to complete what the liturgy already assumes: the Church's lectionary saturates students in the Gospels and the Epistles across the year but distributes the Old Testament more sparingly, so our program ensures the whole of Scripture becomes interior furniture for every graduate. Patristic study begins in Grade 4 with lives of the saints, matures in Grade 8 with St. Athanasius's On the Incarnation, and culminates in Grade 12 with the modern Orthodox fathers — Schmemann, Florovsky, Ware, Sophrony — and a rereading of the Life of St. Antony, which students first encountered in ninth grade. Homiletics scaffolds from oral retelling in the Grammar years to the public preaching of assigned lectionary texts by Grade 12.

Grammar

Grades 4–5 · Narrative Traversal

Students read the Bible as story — memorizing the shape of its chapters, the cast of its characters, the movement of its arc from creation through Christ and into the Church.

Logic

Grades 6–8 · Guided Traversal

Students read the Bible with the Church — through the lens of liturgy, typology, and the voices of the early Fathers. St. Athanasius's On the Incarnation anchors Grade 8.

Rhetoric

Grades 9–12 · Full Traversal

Students read the Bible as future teachers — tracing themes, practicing exegesis, synthesizing dogma, and by the senior year preaching the lectionary before their peers.

4Story
The Story of the Bible & the Gospel of St. Mark

with Lives of the Saints I

The narrative shape of Scripture, retold and memorized. Students learn the canonical sequence, the major characters, and the rhythm of the Orthodox liturgical year, with the Gospel of St. Mark as their close-reading companion. Paired with Lives of the Saints — St. Antony the Great, St. Athanasius, St. Nicholas, St. Macrina — as models of the Christian life.

5World
The World of the Bible & the Gospel of St. Matthew

with Lives of the Saints II

The geography, peoples, empires, and material culture behind the biblical text — Egypt, Mesopotamia, Canaan, Rome, and the monastic world of the early Christian desert. The Gospel of St. Matthew serves as the year's close-reading anchor. Lives of the Saints continues with the early monastic fathers and mothers.

6Liturgy
Scripture in the Liturgy & Luke–Acts

with the lectionary & the Horologion

A year-long reading of the Bible through the Church's lectionary and the Hours, with the Gospel of St. Luke and the Acts of the Apostles as the year's sustained textual study. Students learn how Scripture is distributed across the liturgical year and why the Church reads what she reads when she reads it.

7Pattern
A Christophanic Typology & the Pauline Epistles

The patterns that bind the Testaments — the Paschal lamb and the Lamb of God, the Red Sea and baptism, the Temple and the Body — traced alongside a close reading of the Pauline epistles, where typological reasoning is most explicit in the New Testament. Students begin to read Scripture as the Church Fathers read it: with the whole Bible at once.

8Voices
Patristics I: The Hierarchs in Our Ears & the Johannine Corpus

St. Athanasius, On the Incarnation, with St. John Chrysostom & St. Cyril of Alexandria

A careful, passage-by-passage reading of St. Athanasius's foundational treatise alongside selected homilies from the great hierarchs, paired with a close study of the Gospel of St. John and the Johannine epistles. Students begin formal theological vocabulary: hypostasis, ousia, theotokos, perichoresis.

9Themes
Patristics II: Typology and Figura & the Pentateuch I with the Prophets

Major biblical themes — covenant, exile, kingdom, sacrifice, resurrection — traced through a sustained reading of Genesis, Exodus, and the major and minor Prophets, accompanied by patristic interpreters of these books. Students begin to see the Old Testament as the Church has always read it: Christologically, typologically, and as a unified witness.

10Exegesis
Patristics III: The Craft of Close Reading & the Pentateuch II with the Wisdom Books

The disciplines of biblical interpretation — grammatical, historical, canonical, and typological — exercised on Leviticus, Numbers, Deuteronomy, and the Wisdom corpus (Psalms, Proverbs, Job, Ecclesiastes, Song of Songs). Students work with Greek lexica, consult patristic commentary, and write exegetical papers on assigned pericopes.

11Synthesis
Dogmatics & Apologetics · Full Bible Traverse I

Lossky · Schmemann · Ware · contemporary interlocutors

A systematic account of Orthodox doctrine with reference to contested questions in the modern Christian landscape, including readings from Vladimir Lossky's The Mystical Theology of the Eastern Church and Fr. Alexander Schmemann on liturgy and the secular age. The year's Scripture work is a first full traverse of the entire Bible — every book read in canonical order — now with the dogmatic framework students have been building since Grade 4.

12Homiletics
Preaching the Lectionary · Full Bible Traverse II

Each senior is assigned a sequence of lectionary readings and delivers prepared sermons on them throughout the year before peers and faculty. Training in exegesis, composition, delivery, and the ethics of pastoral speech, accompanied by a second full traverse of Scripture — the ninth and final pass of the BLT arc.

ii.

Humanities & History

The study of history at All Saints is the study of how peoples and places have sought — and often failed — to live well together, and of the particular civilization that Christ entered and that His Church has since traversed.

In the Grammar stage, students inhabit places before they periodize them. A two-year Cities sequence introduces the disciplines of the humanities — historical, geographical, political, economic — first through the city and the local community, then through the cradle of the student's own ecclesial inheritance: the Byzantine Christian world. In the Logic stage, students take a three-year world-history sequence: Ancient, Medieval, Early Modern. In the Rhetoric stage, they take the full Advanced Placement sequence in Human Geography, World History, US History, and Economics.

Grammar

Grades 4–5 · Cities

The Integrated Humanities & Arts Lab: history, geography, politics, and economics taught through places — the local and then the ecclesial.

Logic

Grades 6–8 · The World in Three Acts

A three-year sequence in world history: Ancient, Medieval, Early Modern. Students learn causation, evidence, and the ethics of historical judgment.

Rhetoric

Grades 9–12 · Advanced Study

The full AP sequence: Human Geography, World History: Modern, US History, and the macroeconomic and microeconomic study of exchange.

An introduction to the disciplines of the humanities — historical, geographical, political, and economic — taught through the city, the neighborhood, and the school's own community. Students learn to read maps, ask historical questions, identify forms of government, and observe the basic dynamics of exchange.

An introduction to the Byzantine Christian tradition through its key places, peoples, and institutions — Constantinople, Antioch, Alexandria, Mount Athos, the spread of Orthodoxy to the Slavic world, and the modern diaspora. Students meet their own inheritance as a living historical tradition.

Egypt, Mesopotamia, Greece, Rome, and the world of the New Testament. Students acquire the long memory of the civilization into which Christ entered.

7

Byzantium, the Islamic world, Latin Christendom, and the Christian East under Islamic rule. A study of how Christianity became a world civilization and of how non-Christian civilizations developed alongside and against it.

8

The Reformation, the European encounters with the Americas, the Enlightenment, the slave trade, and the making of the modern state. Students prepare for the AP-level work that follows.

The formal study of how human activity organizes and is organized by space: migration, urbanization, political geography, cultural diffusion, economic geography. The entry point to advanced social-scientific thinking.

10

The period from c. 1200 to the present, read with attention to global interconnection, empire, revolution, and the transformations wrought by technology and ideology.

11

The major periods, themes, and contested historical questions of the American experience from pre-Columbian contact to the present. Students engage primary sources, develop the analytical writing skills measured by the AP exam, and learn to reason about the nation they will soon enter as adult citizens.

Microeconomics & Macroeconomics

The systematic study of exchange, scarcity, and public policy. Both the microeconomic foundations (consumer behavior, firm theory, market structure) and the macroeconomic system (national income, monetary and fiscal policy, international trade) are covered, with students prepared to sit both AP examinations.

iii.

English & Literature

At All Saints, students learn to write with structure and to read with the whole human race. The writing curriculum is built on the Institute for Excellence in Writing — a sequenced training in the classical arts of imitation, paraphrase, and composition. The reading curriculum, which we call World Classics, refuses the quiet assumption that English and American literature alone are worth serious study at a classical school. The great Christian authors of the Anglo tradition — Shakespeare, Milton, Bunyan, Donne, Hopkins, Tolkien, Lewis, Robinson — are central to the program and are read in full. So are the great Christian authors of other traditions: Dante, St. Augustine, Cervantes, Dostoevsky, Undset, Endo. And so are major voices from outside the Christian tradition whose work illuminates the human condition without demanding that students adopt a competing religious frame.

Our guiding principle is that the Orthodox Church is catholic — universal — and her students should know something of how the human condition has been depicted, sung, processed, and narrated across the places God has made. Students read to witness Creation's groaning for the Creator, and to see, through a thousand frames, how God's redemptive breath is drawing all humanity unto Himself. The reading extends well beyond the familiar Anglo canon while holding back from the religious scriptures of other faiths, which belong to their own liturgical settings rather than ours. The full World Classics reading list is published separately and is curated by the English faculty in consultation with the school's broader academic and spiritual council.

Grades 4–5 · Imitation

IEW foundations paired with global fairy-tale, folk, and mythic traditions. Students imitate to learn.

Logic

Grades 6–8 · Analysis

IEW continues; students begin to analyze narrative structure, rhetorical figures, and argument across the global canon.

Rhetoric

Grades 9–12 · Composition

Continued work in World Classics, college-level analytical writing, and a senior seminar in world literature.

The first year of the World Classics program, organized around global folk and fairy traditions. World Classics II · IEW Foundations

Longer narrative and extended world-building. Homer in child-accessible adaptation, selections from Ovid's Metamorphoses, and the Popol Vuh (the Maya creation epic, read as ancient narrative) accompany the year's turn toward the heroic. Tolkien anchors the year — The Hobbit and The Silmarillion stories — alongside Jack London's Call of the Wild, Robert Louis Stevenson's Treasure Island, Kenneth Grahame's The Wind in the Willows, Lloyd Alexander's Prydain Chronicles, and Rosemary Sutcliff's Eagle of the Ninth. IEW continues developing sentence- and paragraph-level composition.

6
English 6

World Classics III · IEW Intermediate

The ancient world. Homer's Odyssey, selections from Virgil and Ovid, Sophocles's Oedipus Rex, Plato's Apology (Socrates before the court), the Epic of Gilgamesh from Mesopotamia, and Aesop's fables accompany a close reading of Defoe's Robinson Crusoe as the year's novel — a sustained meditation on providence and solitude. Historical fiction by Rosemary Sutcliff, Lew Wallace (Ben-Hur), and Elizabeth George Speare places the student alongside the civilizations that produced the New Testament world. Students begin sustained analytical writing.

English 7

World Classics IV · IEW Intermediate

Medieval literatures, read in dialogue with medieval history. Dante's Inferno in selection, Chaucer's Canterbury Tales, Beowulf, Sir Gawain and the Green Knight, and The Song of Roland form the Anglo-European thread. Julian of Norwich's Revelations of Divine Love, Francis of Assisi's Canticle of the Sun, and selections from Hildegard of Bingen bring in the medieval devotional tradition. Students meet Shakespeare for the first time through A Midsummer Night's Dream — the English Renaissance inheritance of medieval mystery plays and Ovidian metamorphosis, read both as poetry to memorize and as the school's first encounter with dramatic blank verse. Cervantes's Don Quixote in abridgment closes the year, alongside Sigrid Undset's Kristin Lavransdatter (opening chapters) as the great modern historical novel of medieval Christendom. Students refine their analytical writing and begin to argue with evidence drawn from the text.

8
English 8

World Classics V · IEW Advanced

Early modern literatures and the theological novel. Shakespeare's first major plays — Julius Caesar and Macbeth — anchor the year, with Milton's Paradise Lost and Bunyan's Pilgrim's Progress extending the theological imagination. Sophocles's Antigone introduces the student to Greek tragedy's questioning of law, conscience, and divine authority. Machiavelli's The Prince — the foundational modern text on the ethics of rule — is read critically, in dialogue with the year's patristic reading on Christian kingship and virtue. Dostoevsky's Grand Inquisitor from The Brothers Karamazov closes the year's theological novel thread, alongside Sor Juana Inés de la Cruz (the Mexican Baroque nun and poet), Pushkin's short fiction, and Molière's Tartuffe. Students complete the IEW sequence.

9
English 9

World Classics VI · Composition

The typological imagination and the theological novel. Shakespeare's Hamlet opens the year as the great existential text, followed by Macbeth read alongside Dostoevsky's Crime and Punishment — two studies of a soul disintegrating under the weight of its own act. Tolkien's Lord of the Rings and Lewis's Space Trilogy follow. The global canon expands with Chinua Achebe's Things Fall Apart, Ngũgĩ wa Thiong'o's A Grain of Wheat, Wole Soyinka's Death and the King's Horseman, Shusaku Endo's Silence (the Japanese Catholic martyr novel), and the political poetry of Mahmoud Darwish. Composition work moves beyond IEW into secondary-school analytical writing.

10
English 10

World Classics VII · Composition

Close reading and the literatures of the twentieth century, in direct dialogue with AP World History: Modern. Shakespeare's Romeo and Juliet paired with C.S. Lewis's The Four Loves opens the year — a formal inquiry into the varieties of human love. The great war-poetry thread follows, matched to the century's catastrophes: Wilfred Owen, Siegfried Sassoon, Rupert Brooke, and Isaac Rosenberg on the First World War, Dylan Thomas and W.H. Auden on the Second. Faulkner's As I Lay Dying, Hemingway's The Old Man and the Sea and selected short stories, Ralph Ellison's Invisible Man, and the full Brothers Karamazov lead the American and Russian fiction. Global voices continue with Constantine Cavafy (the Greek poet of Alexandria — "Ithaka," "Waiting for the Barbarians," "The God Abandons Antony"), Jorge Luis Borges, Pablo Neruda, Yasunari Kawabata's The Master of Go, Lu Xun's short fiction, Tomas Tranströmer, Franz Kafka, Albert Camus's The Plague, and the Palestinian poet Mahmoud Darwish. Students prepare for AP English Language in the year that follows.

11
AP English Language & Composition

Rhetorical analysis and argumentative writing. The year works closely with American rhetorical and literary texts alongside AP US History — Frederick Douglass, W.E.B. Du Bois, Lincoln's speeches, Martin Luther King Jr.'s Letter from Birmingham Jail, James Baldwin, August Wilson's Fences and The Piano Lesson. The American nature-writing and poetry tradition opens wider here: Walt Whitman's Song of Myself, Henry David Thoreau's Walden, Annie Dillard's Pilgrim at Tinker Creek, Emily Dickinson, Robert Frost, Wallace Stevens, Mary Oliver, Marilynne Robinson's essays, and Wendell Berry's essays and poems. The year's diasporic voices — read alongside the American story of migration and belonging — include Constantine Cavafy (the Greek poet of Alexandria and memory), Olga Sedakova (the contemporary Russian Orthodox poet and essayist), and Vassilis Vassilikos (the Greek novelist of exile and conscience). John Henry Newman's Apologia anchors the year's apologetics reading.

12
World Literature: Senior Seminar

AP English Literature & Composition

The capstone. The year is structured to close the World Classics arc and to prepare students for the demands of AP Literature. Shakespeare returns at full maturity with The Merchant of Venice and The Tempest — the first a serious moral inquiry into justice and mercy (the "quality of mercy" speech and Portia's courtroom argument read as a Christian theological text), the second Shakespeare's final romance of reconciliation and forgiveness. The Victorian novel enters through Charlotte Brontë's Jane Eyre — the great English first-person novel of vocation, conscience, and providence — while the modernist novel enters through Virginia Woolf's To the Lighthouse. Russian letters close with Tolstoy's The Death of Ivan Ilyich, a short masterpiece read in direct dialogue with the year's homiletics course. The theological-lyric thread deepens: Milton's Paradise Lost in full, Dante's Commedia, St. Augustine's Confessions, Boethius's Consolation, T.S. Eliot's Four Quartets, and Gerard Manley Hopkins. Two Greek Orthodox voices — Alexandros Papadiamantis (the nineteenth-century short-story master often called "the saint of Greek letters") and Odysseas Elytis (whose Nobel-winning The Axion Esti is modeled on the Divine Liturgy) — enter here, alongside George Seferis's essays on the Greek tradition. Contemporary voices: Marilynne Robinson's Gilead, Seamus Heaney, Derek Walcott's Omeros, Wole Soyinka, and V.S. Naipaul. Samuel Beckett's Waiting for Godot anchors modern drama. Creative nonfiction by Annie Dillard and essays by Rowan Williams close the year. Recent seminar topics have included The Novel in Translation, Suffering and the Sacred, and The Late Style. Students produce a substantial critical essay and present it to the school.

iv.

Mathematics

Mathematics at All Saints is taught as a liberal art — the discipline in which the human mind most clearly apprehends form — rather than as a toolkit or a test-preparation exercise. Our sequence is designed so that every graduate reaches college-level calculus or statistics and has the option to go further.

We use Singapore's Dimensions Math from Grade 4 through Grade 8 because its approach — concrete, pictorial, then abstract — is faithful to how children actually acquire mathematical intuition. In the upper school we move to the textbooks long preferred by mathematicians who teach: Harold Jacobs for Geometry, Paul Foerster for Algebra II, Trigonometry, and Pre-Calculus. Geometry in Grade 9 is paired deliberately with the start of formal Rhetoric and the continued study of Logic, because Euclidean proof is the most accessible form of formal deductive reasoning a young person encounters. Enrichment through Math Olympiad, AMC, and directed problem-solving is available at every level from Grade 5 onward.

v.

Science

Science at All Saints begins with wonder. In the Grammar years, students work with Mystery Science — a phenomenon-based curriculum that teaches young students to observe carefully and ask the kinds of questions that lead to investigation. In the Logic years, students move into Issues & Science, the middle-school program published by the Lawrence Hall of Science at UC Berkeley.

We chose Issues & Science deliberately. It is one of the very few middle-school science programs to receive an all-green rating from EdReports — the independent reviewer of K–12 curricula — for full alignment to all dimensions of the Next Generation Science Standards. The program organizes learning around real-world scientific issues that students investigate as scientists do, building disciplinary knowledge in the service of genuine inquiry. The high-school sequence then follows the traditional Earth-Bio-Chem-Physics ladder, with Advanced Placement options available to students at each level. Laboratory work is central in every year of the program.

vi.

Languages

Every All Saints student studies Greek. Greek is the language of the New Testament, of the Septuagint from which the Church Fathers most often read, and of the theological tradition that shaped the whole Christian East. Every student takes it every year, because it is the common tongue of our study.

The Greek program is a single eight-year continuum that holds Koine and Modern Greek together — the language of the liturgy and the Fathers alongside the living tongue spoken today — so that students read Scripture and patristic prose in the original while also gaining a modern language they can speak. Beginning in Grade 6, students additionally elect a second modern language (Spanish, Arabic, or Chinese), which they pursue through graduation. Strong students graduate trilingually literate: fluent in Greek sufficient to read patristic prose and converse, conversant in their chosen second modern language, and equipped to participate fully in the liturgical life of the Church.

4–8Greek
Greek I through V · Koine & Modern

Five years of sequential Greek holding Koine and Modern together. Students acquire the inflectional system, a working vocabulary, and facility with unvocalized liturgical and scriptural texts. By Grade 8 students read selections from the Gospels and the Divine Liturgy of St. Basil in Greek, and can hold simple conversation in the modern tongue.

9–10
Greek VI & Patristic Greek

Advanced Greek with a focus on patristic prose. Students read substantial passages of St. Athanasius, St. John Chrysostom, and the Cappadocians in the original, preparing the linguistic foundation for the senior year's directed translation work.

11–12
Advanced Greek Seminar & Directed Translation

An upper-level seminar in a patristic or scriptural author, followed by a senior-year directed translation project. Students produce a substantial translation of their own, submitted with a critical introduction and philological notes.

6–12Modern
Second Modern Language: Spanish or Arabic or Chinese

A seven-year sequential program in a second modern language chosen at the beginning of Grade 6. Students prepare for Advanced Placement examinations in their chosen language by the end of Grade 12.

vii.

Logic & Rhetoric

The second and third arts of the trivium are taught explicitly across six years, beginning with Pre-Logic in the late Grammar stage and culminating with Rhetoric II in the early Rhetoric stage. The progression moves a student from learning to think clearly, to learning to argue formally, to learning to speak persuasively and well. Beyond Grade 10, rhetorical training continues implicitly: in AP English Language, in the dogmatic argumentation of BLT Synthesis, and in the Homiletics capstone of the senior year.

5
Pre-Logic I

Monday & Friday, throughout the year

An age-appropriate introduction to clear thinking: the recognition of arguments, the distinction between premises and conclusions, the identification of common informal fallacies. Designed to give Grammar-stage students the vocabulary they will need when formal logic begins.

6
Pre-Logic II

Monday & Friday, throughout the year

Continuation of Pre-Logic I with attention to definitions, classification, and the structure of categorical statements. Students prepare for the formal study of syllogistic reasoning that begins in Grade 7.

7
Introduction to Logic

Canon Press, Introductory Logic (Nance)

The formal study of terms, definitions, propositions, and the categorical syllogism. Students learn to identify, construct, and evaluate formal arguments, building on the groundwork of Pre-Logic and preparing for the propositional work that follows in Grade 8.

8
Intermediate Logic

Canon Press, Intermediate Logic (Nance)

The second year of formal logic: propositional reasoning, truth tables, formal proofs of validity, and the rigorous assessment of deductive arguments. Students complete the logical foundation that will ground rhetorical training in Grade 9 and theological argumentation in the years that follow.

9
Rhetoric I

James Nance, Fitting Words · Canon Press

An introduction to the classical rhetorical tradition: invention, arrangement, and style. Students study the canons of rhetoric and begin to compose short orations of their own. Paired with Geometry in the same year for the mutual reinforcement of formal reasoning across modalities.

10
Rhetoric II

James Nance, Fitting Words, continued

Advanced rhetorical training, including the rhetorical analysis of patristic and classical texts. Students prepare longer compositions and deliver them publicly. The course closes the formal sequence in Logic and Rhetoric and prepares students for AP English Language and the Homiletics capstone that follow.

11–12
Rhetoric in Application

After Grade 10, formal rhetoric continues implicitly across the curriculum: in the rhetorical analysis and composition of AP English Language, in the dogmatic argumentation of the BLT Synthesis course, and in the public preaching of assigned lectionary texts in the senior year's Homiletics capstone.

viii.

Sacred Arts

Iconography, chant, and drama are not ornaments to the curriculum. They are among its central disciplines. An icon is written theology; a hymn is sung doctrine; a scene well played is Scripture made present. In studying them, students learn to make — with their hands, their voices, and their bodies — the same tradition they are reading in BLT and encountering in the liturgy each week.

The Sacred Arts department is organized into three tracks that students pursue in parallel, each running the full nine years. Iconography progresses from canonical copying in the Grammar stage to independent studio commissions in the Logic stage and a senior capstone in the Rhetoric stage. Chant moves from aural foundations to liturgical service by Grade 8 to congregational leadership by Grade 12. Drama begins in sacred-theatre exercises and grows into the school's annual productions — a fall play and a spring musical every year — through which students take up acting, staging, design, and technical direction as disciplines of shared work and public witness.

Iconography

4Intro
Introduction to Iconography

The first encounter with the canonical tradition: reading icons as theological statements, learning the principal types (Pantocrator, Theotokos, feast icons), and beginning simple copying exercises in dry media.

5–7Foundations
Iconography Foundations I–III

Three years of preparatory work: copying, pigment preparation, board and gesso work, and the disciplined study of canonical proportions and gestures. Students produce finished studies by the end of Grade 7.

8–9Studio
Iconography Studio I–II

The first two studio years. Students undertake their first independent commissions in egg tempera on gessoed board, working within the canonical tradition under close faculty critique. Christological iconography is the focus of Studio I, paired with the Grade 8 reading of St. Athanasius's On the Incarnation.

10Advanced
Advanced Iconography

An intensive studio year with expanded commissions and the introduction of larger-format work. Critique and consultation with visiting iconographers are a central part of the studio rhythm, preparing students for the Master Workshop that follows.

11Master I
Master Iconography Workshop I & Capstone Commission I

The first of two years in the senior iconography workshop. Students identify, propose, and begin the substantial capstone commission — typically an icon to be installed in a parish or in the school itself — accompanied by written research into its iconographic and theological sources.

12Master II
Master Iconography Workshop II & Capstone Commission II

The completion and public presentation of the capstone icon, with a final written account of its iconographic sources and theological content. One of the three senior capstone works that constitute the public defense of the All Saints education.

Chant

4–7Foundations
Byzantine Chant I–IV

Four years of foundational instruction in the eight modes of the Byzantine system (the Octoechos), the hymnography of Vespers, Orthros, and the Divine Liturgy, and the principal hymns of the festal calendar. Students develop aural memory, vocal discipline, and a working command of the neumatic notation.

8Service
Chant V · Liturgical Service

Students begin to serve liturgically as chanters for the first time, applying the four preceding years of study in a parish context. The year's musical work is calibrated to the repertoire required for weekly service.

9–11Advanced
Chant VI–VIII

Advanced chant work, including the more difficult festal repertoire, the long Holy Week and Paschal hymnography, and an introduction to the theoretical and historical study of the Byzantine modal system.

12Leadership
Chant IX · Congregational Leadership

Students take up the responsibilities of leading congregational chant in parish settings, with faculty mentorship. By graduation, every student can stand at the chanter's stand with competence and musical authority.

Drama

4–7Sacred Theatre
Sacred Theatre Arts I–IV

Four years of sacred dramatic arts: scripted readings of scriptural and hagiographical narratives, staged recitations at school feasts, and the ensemble disciplines of voice, movement, and presence. The training lays the groundwork for both liturgical participation and the oral homiletics that culminates in Grade 12.

8–12Production
Theatre Production I–V · The Fall Play and the Spring Musical

Beginning in Grade 8 and continuing through senior year, students take part in the school's two annual productions: a fall play drawn from dramatic literature relevant to that year's English and Humanities curricula or the Church's own dramatic tradition, and a spring musical that draws the whole school community into a single collaborative work. Students take up roles across acting, staging, design, and technical direction, and by the senior year many serve as student directors, assistant music directors, or stage managers.

ix.

Physical Formation

Physical education at All Saints is designed as formation, not activity. We ask the body to learn what the mind and soul are also learning: discipline, courage, teamwork, humility, and the stewardship of the gifts God has given.

Our physical program combines team basketball, strength and conditioning, flexibility and mobility training, self-defense, and outdoor education. Outdoor education occurs on a distinct rhythm: quarterly school-wide hikes that include families, alongside longer expeditions in the upper grades. By high school, students elect their own mix of team athletics and individual training.

4–5Foundations
Physical Education: Foundations

Movement literacy, coordination, flexibility, and the foundations of strength. Introduction to basketball fundamentals and to the quarterly family hike tradition.

6–8Team & Self
PE: Team Sports & Self-Defense

Competitive basketball, the other court sports accessible in our facility, and a self-defense sequence taught with attention to de-escalation, personal responsibility, and the ethics of physical force.

9–12Athletics
Athletics & Strength & Conditioning

Upper-school students elect a combination of competitive team athletics (basketball) and a programmed strength-and-conditioning curriculum. Outdoor education weekends and family hikes continue on the quarterly rhythm.

IV
Horizontal Coherence

Nine Years at All Saints

What distinguishes All Saints is not the list of subjects but the conversations they hold with one another in a single year of a student's life — and across all nine. Below is a narrative account of each year, from the first steps in Grade 4 through the senior capstone.

4 Grammar Stage · Story
The year everything begins as story.

The student enters All Saints and meets Scripture as a single great narrative. BLT opens with the Gospel of St. Mark and the arc of salvation history from creation to Christ. In English, IEW composition and world folk and fairy traditions — Russian, Greek, Chinese, West African — teach the student that every people tells itself through story.

The first iconography instruction begins: how an icon is read, who the Pantocrator is, what the Theotokos means. The first Greek letters are learned. Humanities begins with the city and the neighborhood — the student's own place, before any periodization. Mathematics starts the Singapore sequence that will carry through to Algebra I.

Lives of the Saints is introduced through St. Antony the Great, St. Athanasius, St. Nicholas of Myra, and St. Macrina — the great early models of the Christian life. Students memorize prayers attributed to these saints and hear their feast days announced at Morning Prayer, so the saints enter the student's life as companions rather than as remote figures in a book. The classroom of five to six students in the founding year means the student is known by name in the first week of school.

5 Grammar Stage · World
The year Scripture acquires its ground.

The student learns the world the Bible describes — Egypt, Mesopotamia, Canaan, Rome — with the Gospel of St. Matthew as the year's scriptural anchor. In Humanities, the second Cities year introduces the Byzantine Christian world: Constantinople, Antioch, Mount Athos, the spread of Orthodoxy to the Slavic world, the modern diaspora. The student begins to see their own inheritance as a historical tradition that can be studied.

Pre-Logic I starts on Mondays and Fridays: recognizing arguments, identifying fallacies, distinguishing premises from conclusions. Iconography Foundations I begins serious copying work. Lives of the Saints II introduces the early monastic fathers and mothers — St. Pachomius the father of cenobitic monasticism, St. Mary of Egypt, and St. John Climacus of the Ladder.

Math Olympiad enrichment becomes available. The monthly cohort hike begins to shape the year's rhythm. The Greek continuum deepens, holding Koine and the modern tongue together.

6 Logic Stage · Liturgy
The year the liturgy becomes legible.

Scripture is now read through the Church's lectionary and the Hours. The student learns why the Church reads Luke–Acts in the season she reads it, what the Old Testament readings of Lent mean, and why the Holy Week services are arranged as they are. Humanities turns to World History I: Ancient, reading the civilizations that produced the New Testament world.

The second modern language begins — Spanish, Arabic, or Chinese, chosen once and pursued through graduation. Pre-Logic II continues. Iconography Foundations II introduces gesso and pigment preparation; a first small finished study is produced by the end of the year.

The saints become present in a new way this year: students read from the Sayings of the Desert Fathers and the homilies of St. John Chrysostom, whose preaching shapes the imagination of how a saint speaks and writes. This is also the first year students participate in monthly prosphora baking — preparing the liturgical bread for the parish's Divine Liturgy — and in quarterly community service projects with partner parishes in the region. Community service at All Saints begins in Grade 6 and builds through graduation as a sustained discipline of the school.

7 Logic Stage · Pattern
The year the Testaments begin to speak to each other.

Typology becomes the lens: the Paschal lamb and the Lamb of God, the Red Sea and baptism, the Temple and the Body. The Pauline epistles are the year's close-reading text, because Paul reads typologically more explicitly than any other New Testament author. In Humanities, World History II introduces the medieval world — Byzantium, the Islamic world, Latin Christendom, the Christian East under Islamic rule.

Introduction to Logic begins in earnest: Canon Press's Introductory Logic, categorical syllogisms, formal evaluation of arguments. English 7 reads medieval devotional writing, European epic, and Asian narrative traditions — the medieval literary horizon that matches the history. Greek IV prepares the student to read small passages in the original.

Lives of the Saints widens this year to take in the breadth of the Orthodox world: St. Seraphim of Sarov and the Russian hesychast legacy, St. Innocent of Alaska who brought Orthodoxy to the Americas, and St. Nikolai Velimirović of Serbia — saints whose lives testify that the Orthodox inheritance spans every nation. Iconography Foundations III produces the year's most technically demanding work yet. The student begins to see the morning prayer service as text they are equipped to follow and the liturgical calendar as a structure they can predict.

8 Logic Stage · Voices
The year the Fathers enter the room.

This is the year Patristics I begins — and the curriculum pivots. St. Athanasius's On the Incarnation is read passage by passage, paired with the Gospel of St. John and the Johannine epistles. But St. Athanasius does not stand alone: students read festal homilies of St. John Chrysostom, paschal letters of St. Cyril of Alexandria, and selections from St. Gregory the Theologian, St. Basil the Great, and St. Gregory of Nyssa — the great Cappadocian theologians whose grammar of the Trinity the student will carry for the rest of their life.

In Humanities, World History III reads the Reformation and its long arguments with the Fathers — so the student meets Luther and Calvin already knowing Chrysostom on the priesthood. In Greek V, the student reads St. Athanasius in the original for the first time. In English 8, the World Classics V reading includes the early modern dramatic tradition that will feed the Drama track's productions.

In Sacred Arts, Iconography Studio I begins — the first independent commission, in Christological iconography, paired with the year's patristic reading. Chant V means liturgical service: the student stands at the chanter's stand at their parish for the first time. Intermediate Logic completes the formal logical sequence.

This is the year the concert of the curriculum first becomes audible to the student themselves. The student reading St. Athanasius in BLT is reading him in Greek, meeting his historical moment in Humanities, and learning the genre of theological argument in English — all in the same eight months of the same year.

9 Rhetoric Stage · Themes
The year the Old Testament becomes a Christian book.

Patristics II: Typology and Figura. The student reads Genesis, Exodus, and the major and minor Prophets, with patristic interpreters of each. Here the student meets the earliest witnesses — St. Ignatius of Antioch's letters on the way to martyrdom, St. Polycarp of Smyrna on apostolic faith — alongside St. Ephrem the Syrian on Genesis, St. Cyril of Jerusalem's mystagogical catecheses, and St. Paisios of Mount Athos on the inner life of prayer that the Prophets demand. Monastic spirituality enters formally with the desert fathers — the Apophthegmata, the sayings of Abba Macarius, Abba Poemen, and Amma Syncletica.

Geometry begins in Math, paired deliberately with the start of formal Rhetoric — because Euclidean proof is the most accessible form of formal deductive reasoning a young person meets, and it reinforces the rhetorical canon of arrangement. Earth and Space Science replaces the middle-school integrated-science sequence. AP Human Geography is the year's humanities course — the formal study of how human activity organizes space.

The Life of St. Antony is read carefully this year — the text the student first met at age nine, now read as a literary and theological document. The student begins Advanced Greek, reading longer passages of patristic prose. The fall play rehearsal intensifies across October; the spring musical becomes a full production with Grade 9 students in lead and supporting roles.

10 Rhetoric Stage · Exegesis
The year reading becomes a craft.

Patristics III: The Craft of Close Reading. The student works through Leviticus, Numbers, Deuteronomy, and the Wisdom books — Psalms, Proverbs, Job, Ecclesiastes, Song of Songs — with patristic commentators. St. Clement of Rome's first letter to the Corinthians as a model of early Christian exegesis, St. Irenaeus of Lyons on the unity of the two Testaments, St. Athanasius's letter to Marcellinus on the Psalms, St. Gregory of Nyssa on the Song of Songs, St. John Chrysostom's homilies on Job, and St. Augustine of Hippo on Christian teaching. The methods of biblical interpretation — grammatical, historical, canonical, typological — are exercised on assigned pericopes with formal exegetical papers.

Biology or AP Biology anchors the year in science. Algebra II & Trigonometry continues the Foerster sequence. AP World History: Modern reads the last eight centuries globally. Rhetoric II completes the formal rhetorical training. Patristic Greek deepens.

Advanced Iconography expands the student's commissions to larger formats. The student now serves as a chanter regularly and may begin small leadership roles in parish service. Community service deepens as well — what began as occasional participation in partner-parish service projects becomes a regular monthly commitment organized by the students themselves.

11 Rhetoric Stage · Synthesis
The year the student learns to argue for what the Church believes.

Dogmatics & Apologetics, paired with the first Full Bible Traverse. The year's patristic and theological reading is drawn from modern Orthodox thought: Vladimir Lossky's The Mystical Theology of the Eastern Church, Fr. Dumitru Stăniloae on the experience of God, Metropolitan Kallistos (Timothy) Ware's The Orthodox Church, and selected essays of Fr. Georges Florovsky and Fr. Alexander Schmemann on the life of the Church in the modern world. The student meets the contested questions in the modern Christian landscape with the full patristic framework they have been building since Grade 4.

AP US History, AP English Language, Pre-Calculus, and Chemistry or AP Chemistry make this a demanding academic year. Advanced Greek Seminar focuses on a single author read closely. In Sacred Arts, Master Iconography Workshop I begins the substantial senior capstone icon — typically commissioned for a parish — accompanied by written research.

This is the year professional internships begin for many students. All Saints is in active conversation with organizations such as the Catena Bible app, whose work on patristic-text digitization could offer students a rare opportunity to combine data labeling and NLP with deep patristic study and the preservation of the Church's interpretive tradition. Placements are being developed through parish, foundation, and professional networks in areas including publishing, theology, law, medicine, and engineering.

12 Rhetoric Stage · Homiletics
The year the student preaches what they have been given.

Homiletics: Preaching the Lectionary, paired with the second Full Bible Traverse — the ninth and final pass of the BLT arc. Each senior is assigned a sequence of lectionary readings and delivers prepared sermons on them throughout the year before peers, faculty, and visiting clergy. Training in exegesis, composition, delivery, and the ethics of pastoral speech culminates in a public defense.

AP Calculus BC or AP Statistics, AP Economics (Micro and Macro), Physics or AP Physics C, and the Senior Seminar in World Literature. Directed Translation in Greek asks the student to produce a substantial translation with critical introduction and philological notes.

The three senior capstones — the sermon series (Homiletics), the Master Workshop icon (Sacred Arts), and the Senior Seminar analytical essay — are produced and publicly presented. The fall play and spring musical see seniors as student directors, stage managers, and lead performers. The professional internship culminates in many cases in a summer placement bridging graduation and college.

The student graduates having traversed the Bible nine times, read the Fathers across four centuries, argued in formal logic and formal rhetoric, performed in ten school productions, stood at the chanter's stand in parish service for five years, and carried an icon of their own hand into the community that commissioned it. The transcript will show the AP course load and the honors. The student will know the rest.

This is what classical curriculum alignment looks like in practice: subjects in every year meet each other deliberately, not by accident. The work is the discipline the faculty undertakes each summer in joint planning, and it is the single feature of an All Saints education that cannot be replicated by a curriculum package alone.
V
Program Options

Three Days, Five Days, and Extended Care

All Saints Orthodox Academy offers every family a choice of program intensity, so that our families' different working and educational realities are supported. The two core programs, Core and Immersive, share the same curriculum, the same faculty, and the same liturgical rhythm; they differ in how much of the student's week is spent under the school's direct formation. Extended Care through 5:00 p.m. is available to families choosing either program.

Program A

All Saints Core

Three-Day · Tuesday through Thursday

The full academic spine of an All Saints education, delivered in three disciplined days on campus and anchored by structured independent work at home on Mondays and Fridays.

  • On campus T–Th: BLT, Humanities, English, Mathematics, Science, Greek, Logic, and one track of Sacred Arts per year.
  • At home M & F: Structured independent work supplied by faculty — reading, writing, problem sets, practice — with weekly checkpoints.
  • Liturgical participation on the same rhythm as the Immersive cohort; school-wide hikes and productions remain shared.
  • Suited to families already committed to a home-learning culture, and to families whose schedules or distance make a daily commute to San Juan Capistrano impractical.
Program B

All Saints Immersive

Five-Day · Monday through Friday

The full All Saints formation on campus every day of the week — with the independent-work hours of the Core program folded back into supervised classroom and studio time.

  • Five days on campus with the full core curriculum plus extended Sacred Arts time — dedicated studio hours for iconography, additional rehearsal time for the fall play and spring musical, and expanded chant practice.
  • Second modern language at full pace, supervised study hall, and weekly outdoor education or athletics blocks.
  • Mentored independent work held inside the school day rather than sent home, with faculty available for one-on-one consultation.
  • Suited to families who want the full benefit of the scheduled day, and students for whom daily cohort presence is formative.
Extended Care until 5:00 p.m.
Available to both Core and Immersive families, every school day of the year. Extended Care, in future years as our enrollment and resources grow, will carry a suite of electives and student clubs — theatre rehearsals, speech and debate, creative writing, robotics, and additional offerings that will develop with student interest and faculty capacity — alongside supervised homework, quiet study, and drop-in studio and chant practice. Designed for working parents, for students who want an unhurried transition between the academic day and home, and for any family for whom the standard dismissal time is logistically difficult.
The Core and Immersive programs are two routes to the same formation. A graduate of either program takes up the same diploma and meets the same portrait-of-a-graduate standard. The difference is not one of rigor but of how much of the student's week the school holds directly — which is the family's decision to make, not ours.

Humanities & History

Nine years, including the full AP sequence: Human Geography, World History: Modern, US History, and Economics (Micro and Macro).

English & Literature

Nine years of writing and reading through the World Classics sequence, including AP English Language, AP English Literature, and the Senior Seminar in World Literature.

Languages

Eight years of Greek across the Koine and Modern continuum, plus seven years of a second modern language beginning in Grade 6.

Logic & Rhetoric

Six years of formal training: Pre-Logic (Grades 5–6), Introduction to Logic and Intermediate Logic (Grades 7–8), and Rhetoric (Grades 9–10). Continued application through Grade 12.

Sacred Arts

Nine years of continuous instruction across three tracks: iconography (culminating in a senior capstone icon), Byzantine chant (culminating in congregational leadership), and drama (culminating in the senior-year fall play and spring musical). Every student takes all three tracks every year.

Physical Formation

Nine years of physical education, team athletics, strength & conditioning, and outdoor education.

Senior Capstones

Three capstone works are produced in the senior year: a sermon series (Homiletics), a substantial icon (Sacred Arts), and an analytical essay (Senior Seminar). Together they constitute the public defense of the All Saints education.

Portrait of a Graduate

A graduate of All Saints leaves the school prepared to take up a place in the Church and the world — as a student, a neighbor, a citizen, a servant.

Has traversed the whole Bible nine times through nine developmental lenses, and can locate any passage within the sweep of salvation history.
Reads patristic Greek sufficient to work from the original and understands the theological grammar of the Church Fathers.
Serves liturgically and can lead congregational chant, having been formed in the music of the Church since Grade 4.
Has written icons in the canonical tradition and understands sacred image as theology made visible.
Has stood in sacred drama and on the school stage for nine years, and knows the disciplines of collaborative performance from the inside.
Argues with clarity and charity, having studied formal logic, rhetoric, and the preaching of the Word.
Reasons quantitatively through calculus or advanced statistics and knows what mathematical proof asks of a mind.
Has completed advanced laboratory coursework in biology, chemistry, and physics, and has been connected — through mentorship, internship, or collaborative project — to professional scientific and medical networks beyond the school.
Has read widely across world literatures and can locate the human condition in traditions beyond their own.
Has carried real work into the world through professional internships with organizations at the forefront of innovation and the preservation of the faith — including partners in technology, publishing, and the digital stewardship of the Church's tradition.
Takes up the body's disciplines of strength, coordination, and outdoor competence as part of a whole human life.
